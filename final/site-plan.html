Storm Safety & Preparedness Hub

Individual Project Site Plan

Site Name

Proposed Name: Storm Safety & Preparedness Hub

This name clearly communicates that the site is focused on weather-related safety and practical preparedness. It feels approachable to everyday users while still sounding reliable and educational.

Optional domain (for planning purposes only): stormsafetyhub.org

Site Purpose

The Storm Safety & Preparedness Hub will provide easy-to-understand guidance to help people prepare for and respond to hazardous weather. The site will focus on major threats such as tornadoes, severe thunderstorms, flash flooding, winter storms, and high winds.

The website will organize information into simple, actionable sections including:

  • Hazard-specific safety tips and “what to do before / during / after” breakdowns.
  • Readiness checklists and emergency kit recommendations for homes, cars, and small businesses.
  • Plain-language explanations of important weather terminology and alert types.
  • Live or regularly-updated weather information using a public weather API on the home page.

Overall, the purpose of the site is to make storm safety less intimidating and more practical, helping visitors feel confident and prepared instead of overwhelmed.

User Scenarios

These example questions represent the kinds of things visitors to the site might be trying to learn or accomplish. They will guide the structure and content of the three main pages.

  • “What should I do if a tornado warning is issued for my area?”
    A visitor wants a simple, step-by-step guide on where to shelter, what to bring, and how to stay informed.
  • “How do I build a basic emergency kit for storms and power outages?”
    A family or new homeowner is looking for a printable checklist of essential supplies.
  • “What’s the difference between a watch, a warning, and an advisory?”
    A user wants quick definitions so they can react correctly when alerts pop up on their phone.
  • “Where can I see current conditions and a simple 3-day forecast for my area?”
    A visitor wants to quickly check the weather in a way that highlights potential hazards, not just temperatures.

Color Scheme

The color palette is meant to feel calm, trustworthy, and slightly “weather themed” without being alarming. Blues and teals suggest reliability and safety, while a warm accent color can be used to gently highlight important alerts or calls to action.

Primary Accent

Hex: #0B7285

Used for links, key buttons, section accents, and important headings.

Dark Text / Heading

Hex: #111827

Used for main body text and primary headings for strong contrast and readability.

Page Background

Hex: #F5F5F7

Light neutral background color for the page body to reduce eye strain.

Card Background

Hex: #FFFFFF

Used behind sections, cards, and content blocks to create visual separation.

Alert / Highlight

Hex: #F97316

Reserved for subtle highlights, alert labels, or callouts about hazardous conditions.

This site plan document itself uses these colors for the background, headings, accents, and content cards as a preview of the final project’s visual style.

Typography

To keep the site clean and easy to read in stressful situations (like checking safety info during a storm), typography will be simple and modern.

Primary Font
Inter, a sans-serif font family. It will be used for:
  • All headings (h1–h6) with heavier font-weights (600–700).
  • Body text and paragraphs using regular weight (400).
  • Navigation links, buttons, and labels.
Font Treatment
Visual hierarchy will be created by combining font size, weight, and color:
  • Headings: darker color #111827 and accent color #0B7285 where appropriate.
  • Body text: comfortable line-height and size, prioritizing readability on mobile.
  • Buttons and links: slightly bolder and using the primary accent color.

Home Page Wireframes

The home page will introduce the purpose of the site, highlight the most important safety content, and give quick access to current weather information. Two wireframes are planned: one for small screens (mobile) and one for larger screens (tablet/desktop).

The final layout will follow this general structure while incorporating responsive CSS so that content remains easy to scan and interact with on any device.
